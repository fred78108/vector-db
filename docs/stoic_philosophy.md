# Stoic Philosophy: Living According to Reason

Stoicism is an ancient Greek and Roman philosophy founded by Zeno of Citium in Athens around 300 BCE. It teaches that virtue — understood as living in accordance with reason and nature — is the only true good, and that external circumstances like wealth, health, and reputation are "indifferents" that lie outside our control and should not disturb our inner tranquility.

The Stoics divided existence into things within our control (our judgments, desires, and actions) and things outside our control (our body, reputation, property, and the actions of others). The key to a good life, they argued, is to focus entirely on the former and accept the latter with equanimity. This framework — sometimes called the dichotomy of control — is the cornerstone of Stoic practice.

Three Roman Stoic writers survive in substantial form: Seneca, a wealthy statesman and playwright who wrote letters and essays exploring Stoic themes; Epictetus, a former slave whose teachings were recorded by his student Arrian in the Enchiridion; and Marcus Aurelius, Roman Emperor, whose private journal the Meditations was never intended for publication. These three figures represent radically different social positions yet arrived at remarkably similar conclusions.

Stoicism has experienced a major revival in the twenty-first century. Its emphasis on resilience, emotional regulation, and focusing on what one can control resonates strongly in contemporary culture and has influenced modern cognitive behavioral therapy. Books like Ryan Holiday's "The Obstacle Is the Way" have introduced Stoic ideas to a new generation of readers.
