# Coral Reefs: Rainforests of the Sea

Coral reefs are among the most biologically diverse ecosystems on Earth, covering less than one percent of the ocean floor yet supporting roughly 25 percent of all marine species. Built from the calcium carbonate skeletons of tiny coral polyps, reefs develop over thousands of years into structures of extraordinary complexity — the largest being the Great Barrier Reef off Australia's northeast coast, which stretches over 2,300 kilometers.

Coral polyps are small, soft-bodied animals closely related to jellyfish. They secrete calcium carbonate to form a hard skeleton and live in dense colonies. Most reef-building corals depend on a symbiotic relationship with photosynthetic algae called zooxanthellae, which live within coral tissue and provide up to 90 percent of the coral's energy through photosynthesis. This partnership also gives coral its vivid color.

Coral bleaching occurs when water temperatures rise even slightly above normal levels. Heat stress causes corals to expel their zooxanthellae, turning them white and leaving them vulnerable to starvation and disease. If temperatures return to normal quickly, corals can recover. Prolonged heat events, like those associated with climate change, cause mass bleaching events that can kill entire reef systems. The Great Barrier Reef has experienced several severe bleaching events in recent decades.

Despite the threats, reef ecosystems continue to inspire scientific research. Coral chemistry has yielded compounds used in cancer treatment, antiviral drugs, and painkillers. Reef structures protect coastlines from wave damage. And the sheer diversity of life they support — from tiny nudibranchs to reef sharks — makes them among the most captivating environments on the planet.
