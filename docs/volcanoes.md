# Volcanoes: Windows into the Earth's Interior

A volcano is an opening in the Earth's crust through which magma, gases, and ash escape from the planet's interior. Volcanic eruptions have shaped Earth's surface throughout its history, building mountains, islands, and vast lava plains while also posing significant hazards to human populations.

Most volcanoes occur at tectonic plate boundaries. At subduction zones, where one plate dives beneath another, the subducting plate releases water into the mantle, lowering its melting point and generating magma that rises to form volcanic arcs — chains of volcanoes like those of the Pacific Rim's "Ring of Fire." At divergent boundaries, magma wells up to fill the gap as plates spread apart, creating shield volcanoes and submarine ridges.

Volcanic eruption styles vary depending on the composition and gas content of the magma. Low-viscosity basaltic magma produces fluid lava flows that spread over wide areas, as in Hawaii. High-viscosity silicic magma traps gases and can explode catastrophically, producing pyroclastic flows — fast-moving avalanches of hot gas and ash — as in the 1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens or the 79 CE eruption of Vesuvius that buried Pompeii.

Volcanoes also have constructive effects. Volcanic soil is among the most fertile in the world, which is why millions of people live on the slopes of active volcanoes. Hydrothermal vents on the ocean floor, driven by volcanic activity, support unique ecosystems that derive energy from chemical reactions rather than sunlight. Volcanic eruptions have also played a role in shaping Earth's atmosphere and climate over geological time.
