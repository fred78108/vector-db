# Black Holes: The Universe's Most Extreme Objects

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so intense that nothing — not even light — can escape once it crosses the event horizon. They form when massive stars exhaust their nuclear fuel and collapse under their own gravity, though supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies may have formed through different processes.

The boundary of a black hole is called the event horizon. It is not a physical surface but rather a mathematical threshold: once matter or radiation crosses it, escape becomes impossible. The point of infinite density at the center is called the singularity, where the known laws of physics break down.

Black holes come in several sizes. Stellar black holes typically range from a few to tens of solar masses. Supermassive black holes, found in the centers of most large galaxies, can be millions or billions of times the mass of our Sun. The black hole at the center of the Milky Way, known as Sagittarius A*, has a mass of about four million suns.

In 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope collaboration released the first direct image of a black hole — the supermassive one at the center of galaxy M87. Despite their reputation for destruction, black holes play an important role in galaxy formation and are thought to regulate star formation through their energetic outflows.
