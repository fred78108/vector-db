# The Origins and Culture of Coffee

Coffee is one of the most widely consumed beverages in the world, with a history stretching back over a thousand years. Legend holds that coffee was discovered by an Ethiopian goat herder named Kaldi, who noticed his goats became unusually energetic after eating berries from a certain tree. Whether or not this story is true, Ethiopia remains one of the most important coffee-growing regions on earth.

From Ethiopia, coffee cultivation spread to Yemen, where Sufi monks used it to stay alert during nighttime prayers. Coffeehouses — known as qahveh khaneh — appeared across the Arab world in the fifteenth century, becoming important social hubs for conversation, music, and chess. Coffee reached Europe through Venetian traders in the seventeenth century and quickly became a sensation.

The coffee plant, Coffea arabica, thrives in the "Coffee Belt" — the band of tropical and subtropical regions between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn. The two most commercially important species are Arabica, known for its nuanced flavor and moderate caffeine, and Robusta, which is hardier and more caffeinated but generally considered harsher in taste.

Today coffee is the second most traded commodity in the world after oil. The industry supports millions of farmers, many in developing nations, and has developed an enormous culture of specialty roasting, brewing methods, and connoisseurship. From Ethiopian naturals to Colombian washed coffees, the diversity of flavor profiles available to the modern coffee drinker is extraordinary.
