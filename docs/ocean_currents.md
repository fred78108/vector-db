# Ocean Currents and Their Role in Earth's Climate

Ocean currents are continuous, directed flows of seawater generated by a combination of wind, water density differences (driven by temperature and salinity), Earth's rotation, and the shape of ocean basins. They form a global circulation system sometimes called the "ocean conveyor belt" that distributes heat, nutrients, and dissolved gases around the planet.

Surface currents are primarily wind-driven and affect roughly the top 100 meters of the ocean. Major surface currents include the Gulf Stream in the North Atlantic, which carries warm tropical water northeastward and moderates the climate of Western Europe, and the California Current, which brings cold water down the western coast of North America. Without the Gulf Stream, cities like London would experience far colder winters.

Deep ocean circulation is driven by differences in water density. Cold, salty water is denser and sinks, while warmer, fresher water rises. This thermohaline circulation (from the Greek for heat and salt) moves water at depth over thousands of years. The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is a key component of this system; there is concern among scientists that climate change could weaken or disrupt it.

Ocean currents also drive upwelling, in which cold, nutrient-rich deep water rises to the surface, supporting highly productive marine ecosystems. The Humboldt Current off the west coast of South America is one such system, supporting enormous populations of anchovies, seabirds, and marine mammals. El Niño events disrupt this upwelling with cascading ecological and economic consequences.
