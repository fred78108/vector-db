# The Rise and Fall of Ancient Rome

Ancient Rome began as a small city-state on the banks of the Tiber River in central Italy, traditionally founded in 753 BCE. Over the course of centuries, it grew from a kingdom to a republic and eventually into one of the most powerful empires the world has ever seen.

The Roman Republic was defined by its system of checks and balances — two elected consuls, a Senate, and various assemblies shared power to prevent any single person from dominating. This system worked reasonably well for centuries, allowing Rome to expand through military conquest and diplomacy across the Italian peninsula, then into North Africa, Spain, Greece, and beyond.

The transition from Republic to Empire came through a period of civil war and political upheaval. Julius Caesar's assassination in 44 BCE plunged Rome into conflict, ending with Octavian — later known as Augustus — becoming the first Emperor in 27 BCE. Under Augustus and his successors, the Pax Romana ("Roman Peace") brought relative stability to an empire stretching from Britain to Mesopotamia.

Rome's decline was gradual and multifaceted. Economic strain, military overextension, political instability, and pressure from migrating peoples all contributed. The Western Empire finally fell in 476 CE when the Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposed the last emperor, Romulus Augustulus. The Eastern half, known as the Byzantine Empire, survived for nearly another thousand years.
