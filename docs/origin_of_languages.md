# The Origin and Diversity of Human Language

Human language is one of the most remarkable and mysterious phenomena in the natural world. No other species has developed a communication system anywhere near as complex, expressive, or flexible. Yet despite millennia of speculation and decades of scientific study, the origin of language remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in science.

The world's approximately 7,000 living languages descend from earlier proto-languages through processes of change, divergence, and occasional mixing. Historical linguists can reconstruct ancient languages by identifying systematic correspondences among related modern languages. Proto-Indo-European, the ancestor of languages from English and Spanish to Russian and Hindi, was spoken roughly 5,000 to 8,000 years ago, probably on the Eurasian steppes.

All human languages share certain universal features: every language has a vocabulary of meaningful units (morphemes), a set of sound contrasts, and grammatical rules for combining words into sentences. Yet the differences are also striking — in the sounds used, the complexity of verb and noun systems, the order of words in a sentence, and how concepts like time, space, and color are encoded.

Languages change constantly. Over generations, pronunciation shifts, vocabulary evolves through borrowing and invention, and grammar restructures. Languages diverge when communities become separated; they converge when communities mix. Languages die when the last speakers shift to another tongue, and with them goes an irreplaceable window into human history and cognition. Linguists estimate that half of the world's living languages may be extinct by the end of this century.
