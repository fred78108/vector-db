# The Science of Fermentation

Fermentation is a metabolic process in which microorganisms — bacteria, yeasts, or molds — convert organic compounds, typically sugars, into other products such as alcohol, acids, or gases in the absence of oxygen. It is one of the oldest biotechnologies, practiced by humans for thousands of years to preserve food and create beverages, and today is a cornerstone of both the food industry and modern biotechnology.

In alcoholic fermentation, yeasts convert glucose into ethanol and carbon dioxide. This process is the foundation of beer, wine, and spirits. Lactic acid fermentation, carried out by bacteria such as Lactobacillus, converts sugars into lactic acid. It is responsible for the preservation and flavor development in yogurt, cheese, sauerkraut, kimchi, and sourdough.

Louis Pasteur was the first to demonstrate, in the 1850s, that fermentation is the work of living microorganisms rather than a purely chemical process. His discoveries overturned the prevailing theory of spontaneous generation and laid the groundwork for germ theory. The word "pasteurization" — the process of heating foods to kill harmful microbes — commemorates his contributions.

Modern industrial fermentation extends far beyond food and drink. Microorganisms are used to produce antibiotics like penicillin, vitamins, amino acids, enzymes for laundry detergents, and biofuels. Metabolic engineering allows scientists to reprogram microbial metabolism to produce novel compounds. In the emerging field of precision fermentation, microbes are programmed to produce animal proteins — such as casein and whey — without the need for livestock.
