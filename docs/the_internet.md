# The Internet: From ARPANET to a Global Network

The internet traces its origins to ARPANET, a computer network funded by the U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) in the late 1960s. Its original purpose was to allow researchers at different universities to share computing resources and communicate. The first message sent over ARPANET was "lo" — the first two letters of "login" before the system crashed.

The foundational innovation of the internet is packet switching, in which data is broken into small chunks (packets) that travel independently across the network and are reassembled at their destination. This design, formalized in the TCP/IP protocols developed by Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn in the 1970s, makes the network resilient — if one path is blocked, packets find another route.

The World Wide Web, often confused with the internet itself, is actually a service that runs on top of it. Invented by Tim Berners-Lee at CERN in 1989, the Web introduced hyperlinks, HTML, and browsers, making it easy for non-technical users to navigate and share information. The release of the Mosaic browser in 1993 triggered explosive public adoption.

Today the internet connects more than five billion people and underpins global commerce, communication, science, and culture. Cloud computing, streaming video, social media, and remote work all depend on it. Yet the internet's architecture also enables surveillance, misinformation, and cybercrime, raising profound questions about governance, privacy, and the distribution of power in the digital age.
