# Honey Bees: Social Insects and Ecological Keystones

Honey bees (Apis mellifera) are among the most studied and economically important insects on earth. They live in highly organized colonies of up to 80,000 individuals, with a single reproductive queen, thousands of female workers, and a smaller number of male drones whose sole purpose is to mate with new queens.

The division of labor in a bee colony is remarkably sophisticated. Young worker bees perform duties inside the hive — nursing larvae, producing wax, and guarding the entrance. As they age, they transition to foraging for nectar, pollen, and water. Foragers communicate the location of food sources to their nestmates using the "waggle dance," a figure-eight movement whose direction and duration encode both the direction and distance to the flowers.

Honey is produced by bees as a food store for winter. Foragers collect nectar and pass it to house bees, who add enzymes and fan the mixture to evaporate water until it reaches the thick, stable consistency of honey. A single colony may store more than 30 kilograms of honey to survive cold months when flowers are not blooming.

As pollinators, honey bees are critical to agricultural ecosystems. They pollinate almonds, apples, blueberries, and dozens of other crops, contributing tens of billions of dollars to the global food supply. Colony Collapse Disorder — a phenomenon in which worker bees mysteriously disappear from hives — raised alarms in the mid-2000s and has spurred research into the combined stressors threatening bee health, including pesticides, pathogens, habitat loss, and climate change.
