# Marathon Running: History, Physiology, and Training

The marathon's distance of 26.2 miles (42.195 km) traces its origin to the legend of Pheidippides, a Greek soldier said to have run from the battlefield at Marathon to Athens to announce victory over the Persians in 490 BCE, then collapsed and died. The modern marathon was introduced at the first Olympic Games in Athens in 1896, with the distance standardized at the 1908 London Olympics to accommodate the British royal family's viewing preferences.

Completing a marathon demands exceptional aerobic conditioning. The body relies primarily on aerobic metabolism — burning fat and glycogen in the presence of oxygen — to sustain the effort. At around the 20-mile mark, glycogen stores are often depleted, causing the sudden fatigue runners call "hitting the wall." Proper fueling with carbohydrates during the race helps delay this phenomenon.

Training for a marathon typically takes 16 to 20 weeks and involves gradually increasing weekly mileage, long runs to build endurance, speed work to improve pace, and recovery days to prevent injury. The most common training pitfalls are increasing mileage too quickly, neglecting rest, and racing the early miles too fast. Experienced coaches emphasize that the race is won or lost in the first 20 miles of pacing discipline.

The world record for the marathon stands at 2:00:35 for men, set by Kelvin Kiptum in 2023, and 2:11:53 for women, set by Tigst Assefa in the same year. The Boston Marathon, held annually since 1897, is the world's oldest and most prestigious annual marathon. Today millions of recreational runners participate in marathons worldwide, motivated by personal challenge, community, and the satisfaction of completing one of sport's great endurance tests.
