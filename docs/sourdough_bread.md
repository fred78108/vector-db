# The Art of Sourdough Bread Baking

Sourdough bread is one of humanity's oldest foods, made through the natural fermentation of flour and water using wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria. Unlike commercial bread that relies on packaged yeast, sourdough develops its characteristic tangy flavor and chewy texture through a slow fermentation process driven by a living culture called a starter.

Creating a sourdough starter requires nothing more than flour and water. Mixed together and left at room temperature, the mixture naturally attracts wild yeasts and beneficial bacteria present in the flour and the surrounding environment. Within five to seven days of regular feedings — discarding part of the starter and adding fresh flour and water — the culture becomes active and predictable enough to leaven bread.

The bread-making process itself involves mixing the starter with flour, water, and salt, then allowing the dough to undergo a bulk fermentation of several hours. During this time, the dough is periodically folded to develop gluten structure. The dough is then shaped and undergoes a final proof, often overnight in the refrigerator to develop flavor.

Baking sourdough in a Dutch oven traps steam during the first phase of baking, enabling the bread to expand fully and develop a crisp, blistered crust. The result is a loaf with an open, irregular crumb structure, a deeply caramelized exterior, and a complex flavor that no commercial yeast bread can replicate.
