# Plate Tectonics: The Engine of Earth's Surface

Plate tectonics is the scientific theory describing the large-scale motion of the rigid outer layer of the Earth — the lithosphere — divided into a mosaic of tectonic plates. These plates float on the semi-fluid asthenosphere beneath them, driven by heat escaping from the planet's interior through convection currents, ridge push, and slab pull.

Earth's surface is divided into about a dozen major plates and several smaller ones. Where plates move apart at divergent boundaries, magma wells up to create new oceanic crust, forming mid-ocean ridges like the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. Where plates collide at convergent boundaries, one plate typically dives beneath the other in a process called subduction, generating volcanoes and deep ocean trenches. Where plates slide past each other at transform boundaries, friction builds and releases as earthquakes.

The theory of plate tectonics explains phenomena that previously puzzled scientists: why the coastlines of South America and Africa seem to fit together like puzzle pieces, why earthquakes and volcanoes cluster in specific zones, and why similar fossils appear on continents separated by thousands of miles of ocean.

Alfred Wegener proposed the concept of continental drift in 1912, but his idea was widely rejected because he couldn't explain the mechanism. It wasn't until the 1960s, when seafloor spreading was discovered and paleomagnetic data confirmed symmetric striping of the oceanic crust, that the theory was accepted. Today plate tectonics is the unifying framework of the earth sciences.
